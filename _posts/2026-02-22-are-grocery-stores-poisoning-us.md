I was recently waiting in line at the local pharmacy and looking out into the aisles. A wave of disgust began to coat my insides. Almost everything within eyesight seemed to be essentially poison to our health. A whole aisle dedicated to candy and sugar. Another aisle full of cheap crap that was likely produced thousands of miles overseas, and which will likely either end up back in those countries as trash or as microplastics floating in our oceans — inevitably with a final destination of entering our food supply and into our brains. This store had a pharmacist in the back with prescription drugs. I get that many of those prescription drugs are modern marvels that allow us to live longer and have a better quality of life, but I can't help but think these companies have no interest in our health.

I'm willing to go one step further: the more you can avoid the modern grocery mega-mart, the healthier you will be.

So what's the answer for the future? I see a future where our daily shopping routines are for healthy whole foods only. Some local markets are already attempting to stock only whole foods. But just as you can't avoid doom scrolling once you open a social media app, you will have a tremendous challenge eating healthy once you enter the modern mega-mart. I see a future where all non-fresh healthy items — oils, vinegars, spices, canned foods, dried beans, grains, and nuts — are shopped online on an automated basis built around our own personal eating habits. Preferably from vendors that farm locally and with care. For example, I've recently begun ordering all my dried beans and legumes online from Rancho Gordo and keeping my spice supply stocked through thespicehouse.com. By continuing to build out a network of places to supply healthy, shelf-stable ingredients, I can commit to only buying fresh fruits and vegetables locally.

The thought of improving the American diet — and health — while continuing to shop at these mega-marts that have no interest in our well-being is a fool's errand. And sadly, it's the most disadvantaged among us who are forced into eating what is essentially poison: cheap, calorie-dense, ultraprocessed foods. Perhaps in the future we can envision a model where people with higher incomes pioneer this approach of auto-ordering shelf staples in a way that creates enough demand for companies to provide healthier food — and food-cooking education — that will allow lower-income families to break the spell of sickness and obesity.
