Thinking hard back to when I have *actually* tracked my progress on anything! It's actually hard to imagine — I haven't really tracked, like out of my head, written down, tracked anything. When I was in school, the teachers used grades and scoring, and that tracked... well, something (how studious I was?). In college, same thing — I certainly did not track anything on my own, except for maybe how many nights in the last month I went out. After college, I joined the Navy, and again they were probably tracking some stuff for me.

And that brings me to the first time I can actually remember tracking anything on paper for myself. During my second deployment I was really having trouble getting a steady workout *regimen* going. Eventually I found a website that posted daily workouts and I started to gain traction. I had a roommate at the time and we decided to start measuring our bodies once a week. It was so exciting! Each week I would get out my flexible tape and we would stand in our undies taking round measurements of our chest, biceps, waist, quads, and calves. And I couldn't believe it — I could watch my body slowly thicken throughout the weeks as I continued to get stronger each week. But it's undeniable: the fact that we were "tracking our progress" could clearly be tied to results and inspiration to keep working.

Which brings me to today, nearly 13 years later, with the stark realization that I haven't tracked anything on paper since then. But why not? Why is this so uncommon for me? Do other people track progress?

As I was pondering this question I decided to see if I could use my skills as a software engineer to aid in this pursuit. My inspiration came from a recent contract I did where almost everything we planned for was from the perspective of "doing things." I guess that's what people do when they are alive — or maybe more properly — they *want* "to-do." On the furthest abstract spectrum, we could say that people want "to-be"; for example, I want "to be happy," "to be financially secure," "to be a good husband," or "to be a good father." But overall, I think you need "to-do" in order to end up "being" anything, and what you spend your time *doing* will inevitably end up telling the story of who or what you are.

Which brings me to my estate/home management app, *ende*. Ende in Swahili roughly translates to "go." In Swahili, if you want to say "let's go!" you say "twende!" And that's the title of my root page — simply because I know we want to do certain things with each day, so let's go! The idea is fairly straightforward: on the home page you can get a quick glance at whether or not you are tracking progress, based on color-coded blocks next to each doable. The health of each doable is based on "acts." Those "acts" are what we are tracking — just like when we think back to my roommate and me in our hooch in the desert measuring our biceps in our underwear.

Simple acts that, when identified and written down, serve as the foundation for actually tracking progress in any given domain in our lives. You want to eat better? Track your meals. You want to take control of your financial life? Track your spending. You want to enjoy the things you own? Track and maintain your shit.

At the end of the day, well, it comes down to your day — and what did you spend it doing? Did any of the acts you did today actually move you closer to who you want to be?

One more note on this idea of using an app like this to improve your home or your life: there really is no substitute for doing the work. Day in, day out — to get the results, you have to work hard. But I think that, especially in the domain of home life, tracking your progress and identifying the acts needed to get there is how you change your life — and the lives of those around you — for the better.
